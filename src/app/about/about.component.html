It's easy to get carried away thinking about toppings and so on, but more than anything else, when you're eating pizza, it's the crust you're tasting. And not just tasting. Biting into. Chewing. The crust of a pizza is literally its foundation, but it is also its essence.
For all its complexity, pizza dough is built on a remarkably simple formula. Classic Neapolitan pizza is made with only flour, water, salt, and yeast. And while different flours have different properties depending on whether they're made from winter wheat, summer wheat, and other variables, it's the yeast that gives the dough its flavor—primarily through the process of fermentation that takes place while the dough is undergoing its rise.
Of course, every recipe is different. And if you're following a recipe, it's a good idea to actually follow the recipe. That means when a recipe says to let the dough rest for a certain amount of time, give it the full time, even though it might seem like nothing is happening. The reality is that everything—from the elasticity of the dough to its crunch, its chewiness, and particularly, how yeasty it tastes—is being determined during this resting time.
So before we get to our actual five tips, here's a bonus one: If a pizza dough recipe says to let the dough rest 8 to 24 hours (or overnight), plan to use the high end of whatever that range is.
